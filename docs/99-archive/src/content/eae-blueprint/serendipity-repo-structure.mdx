## In Simple Terms

Sometimes mistakes create better results than plans.

We were supposed to build the broadcast system in eae.kr (the public site). Instead, the session was pointed at eae-univ (the private university repo). The entire broadcast engine, game system, and editorial tools got built in the wrong place.

Turns out — that was the right place all along.

The private repo became the **operating system** (source of truth, game engines, editorial tools). The public site became the **publishing front** (where content gets delivered). A natural backend-frontend separation that no one designed on purpose.

Editorial principle confirmed: **deconstruct and recombine — sometimes a better structure emerges.**
</Part1>

## System Architecture

<Accordion>
  <AccordionItem title="1. The Accidental Split">
  What was supposed to happen: broadcast system built in eae.kr.
  What actually happened: broadcast system built in eae-univ.

  Result: eae-univ became the private backend, eae.kr remained the public frontend.
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="2. Why This Works">
  **Backend / Frontend natural separation:**

  eae-univ (Private Backend):
  - broadcast/engine/ — game engine source
  - broadcast/games/ — 6 skillset games
  - university/atoms/ — PWA material atoms (katom JSON)
  - Philosophy originals, editorial tools

  eae.kr (Public Frontend):
  - References eae-univ content via URLs
  - Adds its own categories independently
  - Publishing pipeline: MDX → GitHub Actions → GitHub Pages
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="3. Supply Chain, Not Dependency">
  eae-univ supplies the editorial skillset category to eae.kr.
  eae.kr publishes editorial content alongside its own independent categories.

  This is a supply relationship, not subordination. eae.kr is wider — eae-univ is one of its content sources.
  </AccordionItem>

  <AccordionItem title="4. Private/Public × Local/Global Matrix">

  Exclusive private = eae-univ. Editorial originals. No recruitment.
  Public global = eae.kr. Education broadcast. Open to anyone who finds it.

## Theoretical Background

This accidental architecture mirrors a well-known pattern in systems design: **emergent structure**. The best architectures are often not designed top-down but discovered through iterative work.

The editorial principle at work: originals get deconstructed and recombined. In this case, the "original" was a plan, the "deconstruction" was a mistake, and the "recombination" produced a cleaner separation than the original design intended.

Key insight: **the mistake forced a constraint** (private repo cannot be public). That constraint naturally produced the backend-frontend split that would have required deliberate design otherwise.
